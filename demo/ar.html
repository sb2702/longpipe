Face AR PoC — dummy 3D sunglasses

Throwaway. One question only: do our landmarks carry enough signal to hang AR geometry off? Demo-only, not a production shape. The pose fit is CPU-side (cheap, easy to iterate on); the rendering is real WebGL2 with a depth buffer, in its own context — nothing shared with the SDK backend, since the landmarks come back to the CPU anyway.

model — jeeliz glasses loads their actual glassesVTO meshes (glassesFramesBranchesBent.json + glassesLenses.json, three.js BufferGeometry) so this is an apples-to-apples against their demo: same mesh, our tracking. They're auto-aligned into our canonical face space — scale from their lens x-span to our ear-to-ear width, lens centre onto our eye midpoint — so the sliders only nudge. dummy is the procedural pair, kept for reference.

occluder — a mesh drawn depth-only (writes z, paints nothing), so the far temple is depth-tested away instead of tracking across your cheek. This is the trick their demo uses (occluderURL: models3D/face.json) and it's most of the difference between "glasses on a face" and "glasses pasted on a photo". jeeliz head is their face.json — a full head, so it occludes properly. canonical face is ours, built free from face_topology.json's triangle list, but it stops at the ears so a temple past the silhouette has nothing to hide behind. Set off to see what it buys.

Nothing about the landmark model changes. LandmarkNet stays 2D by design. The depth comes from the canonical face model — one fixed, known 3D face (canonical_face.json, exported from ar-scope/canonical_face_model.obj). Known 3D model points + observed 2D points → pose is the standard AR fit, and it's exactly why MediaPipe's own unreliable z is irrelevant here: it is never consulted.

The fit is POSIT — iterated weak perspective with a depth correction, i.e. true perspective. This replaced a scaled-orthographic fit that was measurably wrong: with perfect landmarks it had 6° of pose error head-on and 14° at 45° yaw, which is what "only okay looking straight at the camera" actually was. POSIT measures 0.00° across that whole sweep and 0.29° under 3px of landmark noise.

It keeps the property that made the maths cheap: AᵀA depends only on the canonical model, so it's constant and inverted once at init. Each of the ~8 iterations is just Aᵀb (a sum over landmarks) plus a 4×4 matvec — a reduction and a tiny solve, i.e. one workgroup if this moves to the GPU. focal is the camera's focal length as a fraction of frame width (0.87 ≈ a 60° FOV webcam); being 200px out costs ~2.5° of pose, so it's worth nudging. The rigid subset (above the jaw) is chosen geometrically, not from a copied index list, so the mouth can't drag the pose around when you talk.

residual = RMS reprojection error in canvas px — the honest read on whether the fit is tracking or guessing. The fit recovers known synthetic poses to ~1e-12px and holds scale to 0.2% under 3px of landmark noise, so a high residual here means the landmarks are the weak link, not the maths. The temples are what make yaw legible.
